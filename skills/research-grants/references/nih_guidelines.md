# NIH Guidelines

## Core Review Criteria

NIH reviewers typically assess:

- Significance
- Innovation
- Approach
- Investigator(s)
- Environment

For many programs, the most scrutinized narrative sections are the one-page Specific Aims and the Research Strategy.

## Specific Aims Page

The aims page should do four things quickly:

- Establish the critical gap or clinical/scientific problem
- Explain why the work matters now
- Present a central hypothesis or objective
- Lay out 2-4 aims that are feasible and logically connected

## Research Strategy

Typical structure:

- **Significance**: why the problem matters and what barrier will be reduced
- **Innovation**: what is new compared to the current state of the field
- **Approach**: the study plan, analysis strategy, risks, and alternatives

## What NIH Reviewers Want to See

- Strong rationale grounded in current literature
- Evidence of rigor and reproducibility
- Appropriate sample sizes and analysis plans
- Feasibility supported by preliminary data when expected
- Clear handling of biological variables and validation steps

## Common Weaknesses

- Aims depend completely on earlier aims succeeding
- Innovation is overstated and not contrasted with prior work
- Statistical plan is thin or absent
- Pitfalls and alternatives are superficial
- Preliminary data do not actually de-risk the approach

## NIH Writing Guidance

- Use direct topic sentences
- State the payoff of each aim explicitly
- Keep the hypothesis testable
- Separate novelty from significance
- Make the approach readable for non-specialists on the panel

## Final NIH Checklist

- Aims are complementary but not fatally dependent
- Significance is tied to a real barrier in the field
- Innovation is concrete rather than promotional
- Approach includes controls, risks, and alternatives
- Rigor, reproducibility, and validation are addressed
