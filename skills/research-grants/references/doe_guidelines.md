# DOE Guidelines

## Positioning the Proposal

DOE proposals succeed when they align clearly with the mission and priorities of the relevant office or program.

Common alignment dimensions:

- Energy security
- Scientific discovery
- Advanced computation
- Climate and environmental resilience
- National laboratory collaboration
- Translation to strategic national capability

## Narrative Priorities

A DOE proposal should make these points clear:

- Why the problem matters to DOE mission outcomes
- What technical advance will be delivered
- Why the team and resources are credible
- How the project will be executed and measured

## Strong DOE Proposal Characteristics

- Explicit alignment to office language and program goals
- Credible technical milestones
- Integration of modeling, experimentation, or systems work where appropriate
- Realistic staffing and infrastructure plan
- Clear relevance beyond purely academic novelty

## Common Problems

- Proposal reads like a generic academic paper rather than a program fit document
- Mission alignment is implied rather than explicit
- Milestones are vague or not measurable
- Facilities and partnerships are underspecified
- Commercialization or deployment path is asserted but not explained

## Execution Guidance

When useful, structure the work around:

- Task 1: foundational method or prototype
- Task 2: validation and benchmarking
- Task 3: scale-up, integration, or transition planning

## Final DOE Checklist

- Program and office fit are explicit
- Deliverables and milestones are measurable
- Team roles are matched to tasks
- Lab, industry, or infrastructure dependencies are described clearly
- Outcomes are framed in DOE-relevant terms
